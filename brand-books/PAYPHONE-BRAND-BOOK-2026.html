00 — Brand Audit

The Honest Assessment

Before we build forward, we audit backward. This is a clear-eyed evaluation of where Payphone's brand stands today — what earns its place, what needs surgery, and what is missing entirely.

B-

Overall Grade: B-minus. Payphone has stronger raw materials than 90% of LATAM fintechs. The whale tail icon is genuinely distinctive. The Explorer archetype is well-chosen. The color system is bold without being juvenile. But the execution layer — how these elements come together in real touchpoints — is inconsistent, and several critical brand infrastructure pieces are either underdeveloped or absent entirely.

What's Working (Keep and Strengthen)

Identity

The Whale Tail Icon

This is a genuinely great logo mark. It carries the ocean/freedom metaphor without being literal. It is ownable, scalable, and memorable. The perception data backs it up: 62% freedom, 19% exploration, 11% solidity, 9% flow. Few fintech logos achieve this kind of emotional resonance. Protect it.

Strategy

Explorer Archetype

Positioning alongside Johnnie Walker, Jeep, and Virgin is exactly right for a fintech trying to own "autonomy" as a category word. It creates natural distance from the Ruler/Caregiver archetypes that banks default to. The "surfer digital" persona gives it texture.

Color

Naranja Payphone #FF6400

This orange is not ING orange. It is not Amazon orange. It sits in its own territory — warmer, more saturated, more Latin American. Against the sea of blue fintechs and green neobanks, this stands out. The violet secondary creates a premium tension that prevents the orange from reading "budget."

Voice

The Four Voice Pillars

Simple, Confiable, Valiente, Humana. These four words do real work. They create guardrails without being restrictive. The tone adaptation system (Motivador + Directo for positive, Empatico + Seguro for negative) is sophisticated brand thinking that most companies at this stage lack.

What's Generic or Weak (Cut or Rebuild)

Weak

The Mission Statement

"Simplificar la relacion con el dinero" could belong to literally any fintech, neobank, or payment app on the planet. Mercado Pago, Nu, Revolut, Cash App — all of them could adopt this statement without changing a single product feature. It is true but not ownable. It needs to be rebuilt around what only Payphone can claim.

Weak

Slogan: "Existe un mundo mas simple"

This is a sentiment, not a slogan. It makes no promise. It names no tension. It could be used by an airline, a meal kit company, or a meditation app. A fintech slogan should make the reader think about money differently in six words or fewer. This one disappears the moment you finish reading it.

Weak

Content Pillar Naming

"Producto, Crecimiento, Marca, Comunidad" are internal categories, not content pillars with editorial teeth. Compare to Stripe's implied pillars: "Internet Economy," "Developer Craft," "Global Infrastructure." Payphone's pillars should signal what the brand believes, not how it organizes its Notion workspace.

Weak

Competitive Differentiation

The current materials benchmark against Yape, PayPal, and Mercado Pago on content strategy but never articulate why Payphone is structurally different as a product. "Zero cost" is a feature. "No paperwork" is a feature. Features are not positioning. Positioning is the reason someone chooses you even if a competitor matches every feature.

What's Missing Entirely

Missing

Investor-Grade Narrative

No positioning statement exists that speaks to capital allocators. No category creation language. No "only" statement. For a company that needs to raise capital, this is the single largest gap. Investors do not fund features — they fund categories and inevitabilities.

Missing

Copy System

There is a voice manual but almost no example copy. No hero headlines. No email templates. No error state language. No onboarding copy. The brand book tells you what Payphone sounds like but never proves it by writing the actual words. A brand without a copy system is a brand waiting to drift.

Missing

Brand-in-Action Specifications

The current materials cover logo usage and color specs but skip every real touchpoint: What does the pitch deck cover look like? What does the LinkedIn banner say? What is on the business card? What does an empty state feel like? These are the moments where brand either compounds or erodes.

Missing

Evolution Framework

No guidance on what changes as Payphone scales from 2M to 20M users, from Seed to Series B. Without this, every growth phase will trigger a "rebrand" conversation that wastes six months and hundreds of thousands of dollars. The brand book should pre-decide what evolves and what stays sacred.

Summary: Payphone has a B-minus brand. The raw ingredients — icon, archetype, color palette, voice pillars — are genuinely strong. But the execution infrastructure is incomplete, the positioning is generic, and the investor narrative does not exist. What follows is the rebuild.

01 — Brand Soul

Brand Soul

The irreducible core. Everything else in this document flows from these four declarations. If a decision conflicts with the Brand Soul, the decision is wrong.

Declaration

Payphone exists to make financial autonomy the default — not the privilege — for every person and business in Latin America and beyond.

This is not a mission statement. It is a declaration of intent. The word "default" is deliberate: Payphone does not aspire to offer autonomy as an option. It works to make the old model — where banks gatekeep access, charge for basic services, and trap people in paperwork — the anomaly.

Brand Essence: Autonomy

One word. Autonomy.

Not "freedom" — freedom is abstract, political, overused. Not "simplicity" — simplicity is a feature, not an identity. Not "empowerment" — empowerment implies someone else is granting power.

Autonomy means you already have the power. You just need the infrastructure to use it.

Payphone is that infrastructure. It does not liberate people. It removes the barriers that made them feel captive in the first place. This distinction matters: Payphone is not the hero of the story. The user is. Payphone is the vehicle.

The whale tail logo captures this perfectly. A whale does not need permission to cross oceans. It does not file paperwork to migrate. It moves because it can. That is the emotional truth of Payphone's brand: you are already free — now move.

Founding Tension

The paradox: Money is the most personal thing in someone's life, but the systems that manage it are the most impersonal institutions on Earth.

Banks were built for banks. Payment systems were built for payment companies. Payphone was built for the person holding the phone. This tension — between the deeply personal nature of money and the cold machinery of traditional finance — is the crack Payphone exists inside. Every product decision, every design choice, every word of copy should widen that crack.

The founding tension also contains a geographic truth: Latin America has 650 million people and some of the highest smartphone penetration rates in the world, but financial infrastructure that was designed in the 1970s. The gap between what the technology allows and what the institutions permit is enormous. Payphone lives in that gap.

Cultural Moment: Why Now?

Three forces are converging that make Payphone's timing structural, not accidental:

Force 01

The Smartphone Became the Branch

In LATAM, 78% of adults own a smartphone. Fewer than 55% have a bank account. The phone is not a channel — it is the institution. Payphone understood this before the banks did.

Force 02

Merchants Rejected the Terminal

TAP to Phone is not a feature — it is a category shift. When a street vendor can accept contactless payments with the phone already in their pocket, the entire POS hardware industry becomes a relic. Payphone is the first mover in LATAM on this shift.

Force 03

Regulation Opened the Door

Ecuador and Central American markets are actively creating regulatory frameworks for fintech. This is not deregulation — it is new regulation designed for digital-native players. Companies born on phones, not in branches, have the structural advantage. Payphone is that company.

02 — Positioning & Strategy

Positioning & Strategy

Positioning is not what you say about yourself. It is the space you occupy in someone's mind when they think about the category. These statements define that space for Payphone's two critical audiences.

Operator-Facing Positioning Statement

For individuals and businesses in Latin America who are overserved by bank bureaucracy and underserved by bank technology, Payphone is the financial autonomy platform that lets you pay, get paid, and grow without a bank standing in the middle — unlike traditional banks and first-generation wallets, because Payphone was built phone-first, zero-cost, and merchant-native from day one.

Investor-Facing Positioning Statement

Payphone is the financial autonomy layer for Latin America's 650 million mobile-first consumers and 30 million underserved merchants — a zero-cost, phone-native platform that replaces bank accounts, POS terminals, and payment gateways with a single app, already serving 2 million users across Ecuador and Central America with capital-efficient unit economics.

Category Being Created

Financial Autonomy Platform

Payphone is not a neobank. Neobanks are still banks — they just moved the branch to an app. Payphone is not a payment processor. Payment processors serve the merchant; they tolerate the consumer. Payphone is not a wallet. Wallets hold money; they do not replace the infrastructure around it.

Payphone is a Financial Autonomy Platform: the complete financial layer between a person and their money, built for the phone, not adapted to it. This category is defined by three structural requirements:

  1. Zero-cost to the user. Not "low fee." Not "competitive pricing." Zero. The platform monetizes through merchant services and value-added products, never by taxing the individual's transactions.
  2. Phone-native architecture. The phone is not a screen that mirrors a backend built for desktop. The phone IS the infrastructure. TAP to Phone is the purest expression of this: the merchant's existing phone becomes the POS terminal.
  3. Monolithic simplicity. One app. One account. Personal payments, business payments, e-commerce, card, API. Not six apps from six brands. Payphone is one thing that does everything money requires.

The Competitor Designed to Make Obsolete

Traditional bank accounts paired with third-party POS terminals.

Payphone's structural enemy is not Mercado Pago or Yape. Those are horizontal competitors fighting for the same customer in different ways. Payphone's real adversary is the incumbent stack: the combination of a bank account (with its fees, paperwork, and branch visits), a POS terminal (with its rental fees, setup complexity, and hardware dependency), and a payment gateway (with its integration costs and settlement delays). That three-piece stack is what Payphone collapses into one app. Every customer who switches from that stack to Payphone validates the category.

The "Only" Statement

Payphone is the only financial platform in Latin America that lets a merchant accept tap-to-pay, generate payment links, manage e-commerce, and issue cards — all from one app, at zero cost, without ever signing a bank contract.

This "only" statement is designed to be falsifiable. If a competitor can truthfully claim all five elements (tap-to-pay, payment links, e-commerce, cards, zero cost, no bank contract), Payphone must evolve the statement. But as of March 2026, no competitor in the LATAM market can.

Positioning Test

Read the "Only" statement to a VC who covers LATAM fintech. If their first reaction is "wait — really? Nobody else does all of that?" then the positioning is working. If their reaction is "yeah, that sounds like a lot of companies," the positioning needs sharpening.

03 — Audience Personas

Audience Personas

Three primary personas. Each represents a real cluster of Payphone's user base. These are not demographic cutouts — they are psychological profiles built around the moment of conversion.

CM
Camila Mendoza
Micro-entrepreneur • 32 • Quito, Ecuador

Archetype: The Builder

Psychology

Camila runs a nail salon out of her home and sells handmade accessories at weekend bazaars. She earns $400-800/month and manages every dollar manually. She is not financially illiterate — she is financially underestimated. She tracks expenses in a notebook. She does not trust banks because banks have never trusted her: the one time she applied for a merchant account, she was asked for three months of formal pay stubs she does not have.

Pain Points

Cannot accept card payments without renting a POS terminal she cannot afford. Loses clients who do not carry cash. Cannot separate business money from personal money without opening a business bank account (which requires a commercial license she has not formalized). Sends invoices via WhatsApp photos of handwritten receipts.

Current Solution

Cash, bank transfers via her personal account, occasional PayPal for international clients. She uses a spreadsheet on her phone to track who owes her. Settlement is manual: she checks her bank app, matches amounts, messages clients to confirm.

Trigger Moment

A customer at her bazar booth says "I only have my card." Camila loses a $35 sale. That night, she searches "cobrar con tarjeta sin datafono" and finds Payphone TAP. She downloads the app and accepts her first tap payment the next morning. No hardware. No contract. No bank approval. That experience — the gap between "I cannot" and "I just did" — is the conversion moment.

Emotional Conversion Need

Camila does not need to be "empowered." She is already powerful. She needs a tool that treats her existing business as legitimate without forcing her through bureaucratic gatekeeping. Payphone's brand must signal: you belong here, exactly as you are.

"I just need to be able to charge people. That is it. I do not need a bank to tell me I am a real business. My clients already know I am."
AR
Andres Reyes
Freelance consultant + side business • 38 • Guayaquil, Ecuador

Archetype: The Optimizer

Psychology

Andres works a salaried job at a consulting firm and runs an online store selling imported gadgets on the side. He earns $900-1,200/month combined. He is technically fluent — comfortable with APIs, payment integrations, and e-commerce platforms. He is not looking for the simplest tool; he is looking for the most capable one that does not waste his time. He has used Mercado Pago, tried PayPal, and built a Shopify store that he abandoned because the transaction fees ate his margins.

Pain Points

Transaction fees erode thin margins on his e-commerce products. Managing separate platforms for his consulting invoices and his store payments creates operational overhead. He wants one dashboard that shows personal income, business income, and e-commerce revenue. Every tool he has tried solves one piece and ignores the rest.

Current Solution

Mercado Pago for e-commerce (hates the fees), bank transfer for consulting invoices (slow settlement), personal debit card for everything. He manually reconciles across three platforms every Sunday night. He has a spreadsheet with 47 tabs.

Trigger Moment

He calculates that Mercado Pago fees cost him $1,800 last year. He searches for alternatives. He finds Payphone, sees "zero cost," assumes it is a bait-and-switch, and reads the fine print. When he realizes the zero-cost model is structural (not promotional), he signs up. When he discovers the API documentation and payment link generator, he moves his entire e-commerce operation in one weekend.

Emotional Conversion Need

Andres needs to feel smart for choosing Payphone, not grateful. He is not a charity case — he is a sophisticated operator making a rational decision. The brand must signal competence, not benevolence. This is the tool that serious operators use.

"I spent more on payment processing fees last year than on my car insurance. That is not a cost of doing business — that is a tax on ambition."
SP
Sarah Park
VC Associate • 29 • Miami, FL

Archetype: The Evaluator

Psychology

Sarah covers LATAM fintech for a $400M growth-stage fund. She sees 30 pitch decks per week. She has developed a sixth sense for "neobank with a twist" and filters those out in under 90 seconds. She is looking for category creators, not feature bundles. She needs to take a company to her Monday partner meeting and explain in two sentences why this one is different. If she cannot do that, the company dies in her inbox.

Pain Points

Every LATAM fintech deck opens with "650 million people, underbanked." She has seen this slide 400 times. She cannot distinguish between companies based on their materials because everyone uses the same language: "democratize finance," "financial inclusion," "mobile-first." She is desperate for a company that skips the platitudes and shows her a structural insight she has not seen.

Current Solution

She relies on warm intros, Twitter/X discourse, and conference sightings to filter signal from noise. She pays attention to founders who write publicly about their market with specificity. She dismisses companies whose websites look like every other fintech template. She forwards the interesting ones to her partner with a one-line note.

Trigger Moment

She reads a LinkedIn post from Juan Diego, Payphone's founder, about how TAP to Phone will eliminate POS hardware in LATAM within five years. It is specific. It includes a number she has not seen. It names the incumbents it will displace. She clicks through to the Payphone website. The site does not say "we are democratizing finance." It says "your phone is your entire financial infrastructure." She forwards it to her partner: "This one actually gets it."

Emotional Conversion Need

Sarah needs to feel like she discovered Payphone, not like she was sold to. She needs materials sharp enough to make her look smart in front of her partners. The brand must signal: we know exactly who we are, and we can prove it.

"I do not need another deck that says 'massive TAM.' I need a founder who can tell me exactly which incumbent they are killing and why the timing is now."
04 — Personality & Archetypes

Brand Personality &
Archetypes

A brand without personality is a logo on a slide. Payphone's personality is engineered to feel inevitable — like the brand could not have been built any other way.

Primary Archetype: The Explorer

The Explorer is driven by the desire for freedom, discovery, and self-sufficiency. The Explorer does not rebel against the system out of anger — it simply builds something better and walks away. This archetype is defined by forward motion, not backward glancing.

Why it fits Payphone: Payphone does not fight banks. It makes them optional. It does not protest the status quo. It builds the alternative and invites people to walk through the door. The Explorer archetype communicates this without aggression: there is a better way, and we already found it.

Brand references in this archetype:

  • Patagonia — built a global business by refusing to act like one. Payphone borrows this: massive scale, founder-soul energy.
  • Jeep — not the fanciest vehicle; the one that goes where others cannot. Payphone serves markets that Stripe and Square have not reached.
  • Virgin — entered every industry by asking "why is this so complicated?" Payphone asks the same question about financial services in LATAM.

Secondary Archetype: The Creator

The Creator archetype channels the builder instinct — the compulsion to make something from nothing, to turn a vision into a tangible product. While the Explorer provides direction, the Creator provides craft.

Why it fits Payphone: Payphone's product is not a reskin of existing banking infrastructure. It was engineered from scratch to be phone-native. TAP to Phone did not exist before Payphone built it for this market. The Creator archetype gives the brand permission to be proud of its craft without being arrogant about it.

Brand references:

  • Stripe — developer-first, obsessively well-built, quiet confidence in craft. Payphone's API and developer tools carry this energy.
  • Dyson — "everyone else accepted that vacuums had to use bags." Payphone asks: "why does accepting a card payment require a separate piece of hardware?"

Each axis runs from 0 to 100. These scores define where Payphone sits on critical brand personality dimensions.

Leans serious but never stiff. Payphone manages money — humor is welcome in social content but absent in transactional and investor contexts. Think: a witty friend who happens to be a financial advisor, not a comedian doing finance bits.

Payphone is bold. The orange demands it. The zero-cost positioning demands it. TAP to Phone demands it. But bold is not loud. Payphone does not shout — it states. The difference is the difference between a press release and a manifesto.

Heavily innovative, but innovation expressed through outcome, not jargon. Payphone never says "leveraging cutting-edge technology." It says "your phone is now a POS terminal." Show the result, not the process.

Warm. The "Humana" voice pillar requires it. But warm in the way a capable doctor is warm — empathetic and reassuring, not sentimental. Payphone handles people's money. Warmth builds trust; sentimentality erodes it.

Three Cultural References

Reference 01

Anthony Bourdain's "Parts Unknown"

Bourdain made the unfamiliar feel essential. He walked into places most travel shows ignored and revealed depth where others saw simplicity. Payphone does the same: it enters markets that Stripe overlooks and reveals massive, sophisticated demand. The brand tone should carry Bourdain's quality: curious, specific, never condescending.

Reference 02

Dieter Rams' Design Principles

"Good design is as little design as possible." Payphone's zero-cost, one-app philosophy is the financial equivalent of Rams' design ethos. Remove every unnecessary element. What remains is pure function. The brand should feel like a Braun product: nothing extra, nothing missing.

Reference 03

Surf Culture's Relationship with the Ocean

Surfers do not conquer the ocean; they read it and move with it. This is the energetic truth of the whale tail brand mark and the "surfer digital" persona. Payphone does not fight the financial system — it reads the currents and builds products that move with them. The brand should carry surf culture's blend of calm and intensity: relaxed on the surface, deeply skilled underneath.

05 — Verbal Identity

Verbal Identity

The words Payphone uses — and refuses to use — are as much a part of the brand as the whale tail. This section defines the verbal system with enough precision to write any piece of Payphone communication without guessing.

Name Mythology

"Payphone" is a deliberate paradox. The payphone — the physical object — was the original democratic communication technology. It stood on street corners in every neighborhood, available to anyone with a coin. It did not ask for your credit score. It did not require an account. You picked it up, put in your money, and connected. It was public infrastructure for private conversations.

The name carries this history forward into finance. A payphone on a street corner in Quito in 1995 and the Payphone app on a smartphone in 2026 share the same structural commitment: anyone can use it, the barrier is effectively zero, and the technology disappears behind the outcome. You do not think about the engineering of a phone call; you think about the person on the other end. Payphone wants the same invisibility for financial transactions: you do not think about the payment; you think about what you just bought or sold.

There is a third layer. The name "Payphone" is English in a Spanish-speaking market. This is intentional. It signals global ambition without abandoning local identity. It reads naturally in both languages. It is short enough to work as a verb ("payphone me the money"), a noun ("I use Payphone"), and a brand ("built by Payphone"). Most importantly, it is memorable precisely because of the slight cognitive dissonance: why would a fintech app name itself after an obsolete piece of technology? That question is the hook. The answer is the brand.

Four Voice Principles

01. Simple

Payphone speaks in plain language. Not dumbed-down language — plain language. There is a difference. Dumbed-down language assumes the reader is not smart enough. Plain language respects the reader's time enough to be clear.

Do this

"You can accept card payments with your phone. No terminal needed."

"Send a payment link. Get paid in seconds."

"Your money, one app. That is Payphone."

Not this

"Our cutting-edge NFC-enabled solution facilitates contactless payment acceptance."

"Leverage our omnichannel payment infrastructure to optimize your cash flow."

"Payphone's holistic financial ecosystem empowers users with seamless transactions."

02. Confident

Payphone makes statements, not suggestions. The brand has earned the right to be direct because the product delivers on the claims. Confidence is not arrogance — arrogance makes claims without evidence; confidence makes claims and then proves them.

Do this

"2 million people already use Payphone. Here is why."

"TAP to Phone replaces your POS terminal. Permanently."

"Zero fees. Not a promotion. The model."

Not this

"We believe Payphone could potentially help some users reduce their costs."

"We are trying to build a better way to handle payments."

"We think our approach might disrupt the traditional banking model."

03. Brave

Payphone names the thing. If the banking system is broken, Payphone says so. If a feature replaces an incumbent product, Payphone says which one. Bravery in brand voice means specificity — the willingness to take a position that not everyone will agree with.

Do this

"Banks charge you to hold your own money. We do not. That is the difference."

"POS terminals are a $12 billion industry built on renting you something your phone already does."

"The biggest barrier to financial inclusion in LATAM is not poverty. It is paperwork."

Not this

"We offer a competitive alternative to traditional financial services."

"There are many ways to handle payments, and Payphone is one of them."

"We complement existing banking infrastructure with additional digital options."

04. Human

Payphone is built by people for people. The voice reflects this. It uses first person ("we built this because..."). It acknowledges imperfection. It talks to users the way a smart friend talks about money: honestly, without jargon, and with genuine interest in the other person's situation.

Do this

"We built TAP to Phone because we watched a vendor lose a sale for not having a terminal. That felt wrong."

"Your transfer is on its way. If anything looks off, we are here."

"Running a business is hard enough. Payments should be the easy part."

Not this

"Dear valued user, your transaction has been processed successfully."

"Payphone is committed to providing industry-leading customer satisfaction."

"Please be advised that your account balance has been updated accordingly."

Tone by Context

Investor Pitch

Tone: Confident + Precise. Every sentence should make the investor feel like they are learning something. No filler. No "massive TAM" slides without specifics.

Sample Lines

"Payphone processes $X million monthly across Ecuador and Central America at zero cost to the user. The unit economics work because we are not subsidizing growth — we monetize through merchant services at 40% gross margins."

"TAP to Phone is not a feature. It is a category shift. Every phone sold in LATAM is a POS terminal that does not know it yet. Payphone is the activation layer."

"We are not building a neobank. Neobanks are still banks. We are building the financial infrastructure layer that makes banks optional."

In-Product

Tone: Clear + Calm. The user is handling their money. Every word must reduce anxiety, never add to it. Use active voice. Name the action and the result.

Sample Lines

"Payment sent. Maria will receive $25.00 in seconds."

"Your payment link is ready. Share it anywhere — WhatsApp, email, Instagram."

"You earned $340 this week through Payphone. Here is the breakdown."

Social Media

Tone: Sharp + Culturally Aware. Social is where Payphone's personality shows most. The brand can be witty here, but wit always serves a point. Never post for the algorithm; post because you have something worth saying.

Sample Lines

"Your POS terminal has a monthly rental fee. Your phone does not. Choose wisely."

"The average bank requires 14 documents to open a merchant account. Payphone requires a phone number."

"Someone just accepted their first card payment using only their phone. No hardware. No contract. Just Payphone and five seconds."

Error States

Tone: Honest + Reassuring. Something went wrong. Acknowledge it. Explain it. Fix it. Never blame the user. Never hide behind vague language.

Sample Lines

"That payment did not go through. The most common reason is insufficient funds on the sender's card. Want to try a different method?"

"We are having trouble connecting right now. Your money is safe — nothing was charged. Try again in a moment."

"This link has expired. Payment links last 24 hours. Create a new one — it takes three seconds."

Crisis Communication

Tone: Direct + Accountable. No corporate hedging. State what happened. State what you are doing. State when you will update. Do not speculate. Do not minimize.

Sample Lines

"At 2:15 PM today, some users experienced delays in payment processing. The issue was caused by [specific reason]. All delayed payments have now been processed. No funds were lost."

"We identified a security issue affecting [specific scope]. We have already [specific action taken]. If your account was affected, you will receive a direct notification within the hour."

"We made a mistake. [What happened.] Here is what we are doing to fix it and prevent it from happening again. We will post an update by [specific time]."

02 — About Page First Paragraph

Payphone started with a question that felt too simple to be interesting: why does accepting a card payment require a separate piece of hardware? It is 2026. Your phone has more computing power than the Apollo guidance system. It has NFC. It has biometric security. It has a camera that can read QR codes in low light at ten feet. 07 — Visual Identity Direction

Visual Identity Direction

This section is a designer brief. It is specific enough to execute against and opinionated enough to prevent drift. If a visual decision is not covered here, default to the simplest option that honors the brand personality.

Color System

Payphone's color system is built on contrast and warmth. The primary palette is intentionally small — two colors that do 90% of the work. The secondary palette adds range without adding noise.

Primary Colors (Use Everywhere)

Naranja Payphone
#FF6400 / RGB 255, 100, 0

The brand's primary identifier. Used for CTAs, active states, key headlines, the logo mark, and any element that says "this is Payphone." It is warm, saturated, and energetic without tipping into aggressive. Never use at less than 60% opacity — diluted orange reads as salmon, which is a different brand entirely.

Oscuro Payphone
#222222 / RGB 34, 34, 34

The brand's grounding color. Used for backgrounds in dark mode, body text, and as the primary background for investor-facing materials. It is not black (#000000) — pure black is harsh and reads as "tech default." #222222 has enough warmth to feel intentional. Use for all dark backgrounds, never #000000.

Secondary Colors (Use Selectively)

Violeta Payphone
#5A00B4 / RGB 90, 0, 180

The premium signal. Violet creates depth and sophistication. Used for secondary CTAs, data visualization accent, investor materials accent, and gradient endpoints. It prevents the orange from reading "budget" by adding a luxury counterpoint. Never use as a background color for text — accessibility contrast ratios fail against most text colors.

Amarillo Payphone
#FFA000 / RGB 255, 160, 0

The energy accent. Amber-gold that bridges orange and yellow. Used for highlights, notification badges, success states, and warm gradient endpoints. It should feel like late-afternoon sunlight. Never use for body text or large text blocks — it lacks sufficient contrast against white backgrounds.

Blanco Payphone
#FFFFFF / RGB 255, 255, 255

Clean white for light mode backgrounds, cards, and containers. Use generous white space — it is the brand's most important "color" because it gives the orange room to breathe. Payphone designs should never feel crowded.

Gradient System

Sunset Gradient
#FF6400 to #FFA000 / 135deg

Primary gradient. Use for hero sections, CTAs, feature highlights, and brand moments. This is the "warm" gradient — it evokes the transition from afternoon to golden hour. It should feel aspirational and inviting.

Electric Gradient
#FF6400 to #5A00B4 / 135deg

Premium gradient. Use for investor materials, pitch deck accents, and elevated brand moments. This is the "depth" gradient — it signals sophistication and ambition. Reserve for contexts where Payphone is speaking to capital or partners, not to end users.

Dawn Gradient
#FFA000 to #FF6400 to #5A00B4 / 135deg

Full spectrum gradient. Use sparingly — only for major brand moments like launch campaigns, year-in-review materials, or event branding. This is the "everything" gradient. It should feel like a sunrise over ocean.

Typography

Primary Typeface: Aeonik

Aeonik is a geometric sans-serif that balances warmth with precision. Its rounded terminals prevent it from feeling clinical (unlike Helvetica), while its consistent stroke width keeps it from feeling playful (unlike Circular). It is the typographic equivalent of Payphone's brand personality: approachable authority.

Weights in use: Regular (400) for body text, Medium (500) for subheadings and emphasis, Bold (700) for headlines and CTAs. Light (300) for large display text only — never for body copy.

Secondary Typeface: System Sans-Serif Stack

For contexts where Aeonik is not available (email, third-party platforms, user-generated templates): use the system sans-serif stack: -apple-system, BlinkMacSystemFont, 'Segoe UI', Helvetica, Arial, sans-serif. Never use a decorative or serif fallback.

Three Non-Negotiable Typography Rules

Rule 01

Never stack more than three type sizes on a single screen or layout. If you need a fourth, you have a hierarchy problem, not a type problem. Reduce content or restructure the layout.

Rule 02

Headlines are always sentence case, never all-caps. All-caps reads as shouting. Payphone states; it does not shout. The only exception is labels and micro-text (tags, section numbers, metadata), where all-caps with wide letter-spacing serves a structural purpose.

Rule 03

Body text line-height is always 1.6-1.8x the font size. Payphone's brand is built on breathing room. Tight leading makes text feel anxious — the opposite of the brand's emotional target. When in doubt, add more space.

Logo Direction

The Whale Tail Icon

The whale tail is Payphone's most valuable brand asset. It is abstract enough to scale across contexts (app icon, favicon, watermark, physical signage) and specific enough to be immediately recognizable. The tail represents a whale mid-dive — the moment of descent into the ocean, which metaphorically maps to diving into financial autonomy.

Wordmark: "payphone"

The wordmark is set in lowercase Aeonik Bold. Lowercase is a deliberate choice: it communicates accessibility and approachability while the weight prevents it from reading as passive. The wordmark is always rendered as one word, never "Pay Phone" or "PayPhone." Casing rules: "payphone" in the logo, "Payphone" at the start of sentences, never "PAYPHONE" in all-caps.

6 Logo Misuse Rules

Misuse 01

Never rotate the whale tail. The tail points upward and to the right. Rotating it changes the meaning: a downward tail implies sinking, not diving. The direction is non-negotiable.

Misuse 02

Never change the logo colors. The whale tail is Naranja Payphone or white (on dark backgrounds). Never blue, never green, never grayscale. Brand partnerships may show the logo in white; they may never recolor it to match partner brand palettes.

Misuse 03

Never add effects to the logo. No drop shadows, no glows, no gradients applied to the logo mark itself (gradients may appear behind it as background). No 3D rendering. The logo is flat by design.

Misuse 04

Never place the logo on busy backgrounds. The logo requires a minimum clear zone equal to the height of the whale tail icon on all four sides. If the background is photographic, place the logo on a solid color card or overlay.

Misuse 05

Never separate the icon from the wordmark when both are present. The whale tail and "payphone" text maintain a fixed spatial relationship. They may be used independently (icon-only for app icons; wordmark-only for inline text), but when used together, spacing ratios are locked.

Misuse 06

Never render the logo below 24px height in digital or 8mm in print. Below these thresholds, the whale tail's curves lose definition and the wordmark becomes illegible. At small sizes, use the icon-only variant.

Imagery Direction

Three-Word Filter

Every image used in Payphone communications must pass through a three-word filter: Authentic. Dynamic. Warm. If an image does not satisfy all three, it does not ship.

  • Authentic: Real people in real environments. Not studio-lit models pretending to use phones. Not stock photos of "diverse groups laughing at a laptop." Real merchants in real markets. Real hands holding real phones. Real transactions in real light.
  • Dynamic: Images should convey motion, energy, or the moment just before or after an action. A hand reaching for a phone. A merchant mid-transaction. A crowd moving through a street market. Never static posed portraits. Never "person staring at camera with arms crossed."
  • Warm: Color grading should lean warm. Golden hour tones. Amber highlights. Shadows that trend purple-gray, not blue-gray. This aligns with the Naranja/Violeta palette and prevents the brand from feeling clinical or cold.

Subjects

Prioritize these subjects in brand photography:

  1. Hands interacting with phones (payment moments, tapping, sending links)
  2. Small merchants in their natural environment (market stalls, home salons, street food, repair shops)
  3. Urban Latin American streetscapes (Quito, Guayaquil, Guatemala City, San Salvador)
  4. Close-up product shots (app screens showing real UI, not mockups)
  5. Ocean and water imagery (used sparingly, for brand-level campaigns, never for product marketing)

Banned Stock Photo Types

Never use these

The "diverse boardroom": Six people of carefully balanced demographics standing around a glass table with laptops. This is the visual equivalent of "synergy."

The "happy phone user": Person alone on a solid background, smiling at their phone screen. No context, no story, no environment. Visually empty.

The "fintech city skyline": Generic city skyline with blue overlay and floating data points. This screams "we could not think of an image so we used the first result on Shutterstock."

Behind her hands, blurred but visible, are rows of colorful produce and the motion of other vendors. Afternoon light. Warm color grade. Data Visualization Aesthetic

Charts and data displays use the following rules:

  • Primary data series: Naranja Payphone (#FF6400) at full opacity
  • Secondary data series: Violeta Payphone (#5A00B4) at 80% opacity
  • Tertiary data series: Amarillo Payphone (#FFA000) at 70% opacity
  • Grid lines: #E8E8E8 (light mode) or rgba(255,255,255,0.1) (dark mode)
  • Labels: #666666 (light mode) or #999999 (dark mode), Aeonik Regular 12px
  • Never use red/green for positive/negative. Instead, use Naranja for positive and a neutral gray (#999999) for negative. Red/green color coding fails for colorblind users and triggers unintended emotional responses in financial contexts.
  • Animate data on load. Charts should draw from left to right or bottom to top. Static data feels dead. Movement is a core brand value.

09 — Brand Don'ts

Brand Don'ts

Fifteen specific, opinionated prohibitions. These are not suggestions. They are walls. Every one exists because someone, somewhere, at some company, made this mistake and diluted their brand. Payphone will not.

01
Never say "democratize." This word has been used by every fintech, edtech, healthtech, and proptech company founded since 2015. It means nothing anymore. Payphone does not "democratize finance." Payphone makes financial tools available to everyone at zero cost. Say that instead. Be specific.
02
Never use blue as a brand color. Blue is the color of banks. It is the color of trust in a banking context. Payphone is not a bank. Using blue — even as an accent, even in a one-off campaign — subconsciously positions Payphone in the category it is trying to replace. Orange is the antidote to blue. Stay in character.
03
Never open a sentence with "At Payphone, we..." This construction is the copywriting equivalent of clearing your throat before speaking. It adds no information and delays the point. Start with the point. "Your phone is now a POS terminal" is better than "At Payphone, we have developed a feature that allows your phone to function as a POS terminal." The sole exception is Juan Diego's first-person LinkedIn posts, where "At Payphone, we..." can work once per post as a pivot.
04
Never use the word "users" in user-facing copy. People are not "users." They are people, customers, merchants, builders, entrepreneurs. "Users" is a developer term that leaked into marketing and never left. In internal documents and analytics, "users" is fine. In anything a customer reads, it is banned.
05
Never put the logo on a gradient background without a solid-color container. The whale tail has curves. Gradients have color transitions. Placing one on top of the other creates visual noise that makes both elements weaker. If the background is a gradient, place the logo inside a solid-color rectangle (white or #222222) with padding.
06
Never use more than three emojis in a single social post. Emojis are seasoning, not the meal. Three is the maximum. Zero is often the right number. When used, emojis should replace a bullet point or add tone — never replace a word. "Send money instantly" is not improved by "Send money instantly."
07
Never compare Payphone to competitors by name in public-facing materials. Naming competitors elevates them. Payphone compares itself to the status quo (banks, POS terminals, paper invoices), not to other fintechs. Internally, competitive analysis is rigorous. Externally, competitors do not exist. Payphone exists in its own category.
08
Never use stock illustrations of floating isometric devices. The "phone floating at 30 degrees with app screenshots and decorative circles" visual is the stock illustration equivalent of clip art. It signals "we did not invest in real creative." Use actual product screenshots, photography, or abstract brand graphics. Never a floating phone.
09
Never write "we are excited to announce." If the announcement is exciting, the content will convey that. Telling people you are excited is a meta-emotion that adds nothing. Start with the announcement. "TAP to Phone is live in Guatemala" is better than "We are excited to announce that TAP to Phone is now available in Guatemala."
10
Never use all-caps for headlines in marketing materials. All-caps is visually aggressive and reduces readability by 13-18% (per multiple typography studies). Payphone's voice is confident, not loud. Sentence case for all headlines, always. The only acceptable all-caps usage is for micro-labels (section numbers, tags, metadata) at 12px or smaller with wide letter-spacing.
11
Never use the whale tail icon as a decorative pattern or wallpaper. Repeating the logo as a tiled pattern cheapens it. The whale tail appears once per composition. It is a signature, not a texture. If you need a brand texture, use the gradient system or the ocean photography direction.
12
Never promise specific financial outcomes in marketing copy. "Earn more with Payphone" is a promise. "Accept payments you were missing" is a capability. Payphone describes what its tools do and lets the user draw the conclusion. This is both a brand principle and a regulatory requirement in financial services marketing.
13
Never use serif fonts in any Payphone material. Serifs signal tradition, heritage, and established institutions. Payphone is building the replacement for those institutions. Aeonik (or the system sans-serif stack) is used everywhere. No exceptions. No "just for the pitch deck." No "this one feels more premium."
14
Never publish content without a clear next action. Every piece of Payphone content — every post, every email, every page — must answer the question: "What should the reader do after consuming this?" If the answer is "nothing," the content should not exist. Content without a CTA is a monologue. Payphone has conversations.
15
Never translate English copy word-for-word into Spanish (or vice versa). Payphone operates bilingually. The Spanish and English versions of any piece of copy should convey the same meaning with the same emotional impact, but they are written independently, not translated. "Your phone is your entire financial infrastructure" does not word-for-word translate well. The Spanish version should be crafted as its own sentence that lands with the same force for a native speaker. Translators adapt intent; they do not swap words.
10 — Brand Evolution Rules

Brand Evolution Rules

A brand that cannot evolve dies. A brand that evolves without rules dies faster. This section pre-decides what changes, what stays, and how to make the call.

Evolution by Stage

Seed Stage (Current: 2M users, pre-Series A)

Brand focus: Establish category ("Financial Autonomy Platform"), build founder credibility, prove product-market fit through brand narrative.

What changes at this stage:

  • Messaging emphasis shifts based on which product (Personal, Business, TAP, E-Commerce) shows strongest traction
  • Tone in social channels is experimental — test what resonates with early adopters
  • Visual identity may be applied with some flexibility as the team is small and moving fast
  • Investor positioning is refined with each pitch meeting based on what lands

What stays fixed: Logo, color palette, core voice principles, brand essence (Autonomy), category claim.

Series A Stage (Target: 5-10M users)

Brand focus: Scale awareness, formalize brand operations, build brand equity that compounds.

What changes at this stage:

  • Brand guidelines become mandatory (not suggested) for all external communications
  • Content cadence increases and requires a formal editorial calendar
  • Visual identity system expands: introduce motion design principles, video brand guidelines, event branding standards
  • Sub-brand language may be needed for product lines (Payphone Business, Payphone TAP) — but architecture remains monolithic
  • Paid media creative is developed and requires brand-specific ad templates
  • PR and earned media strategy formalizes — press kit, spokesperson training, media relationships

What stays fixed: Everything from Seed, plus: verbal identity system, imagery direction, brand don'ts, positioning statements (though proof points update with new metrics).

Series B Stage (Target: 20M+ users, multi-country)

Brand focus: Category leadership, international expansion, brand as competitive moat.

What changes at this stage:

  • Brand localization for new markets (visual and verbal adaptation, not translation)
  • Potential brand refresh (not rebrand): updated type scale, refined color weights, evolved illustration style — all within the existing system
  • Co-branding guidelines become critical as partnership volume increases
  • Brand architecture may evolve if Payphone launches products that serve meaningfully different audiences (e.g., Payphone Capital, Payphone Enterprise) — but the whale tail remains the master brand
  • Employer brand becomes a distinct workstream

What stays fixed: Logo mark (whale tail), Naranja Payphone (#FF6400), brand essence (Autonomy), founding tension, voice principles. These are the eternal elements.

Sacred Elements (Never Change)

Sacred 01

The Whale Tail Icon

This icon is the brand. It can be refined (subtle curve adjustments, optical corrections) but never redesigned, replaced, or rotated. The whale tail is to Payphone what the swoosh is to Nike: the single visual element that carries the entire brand even without the wordmark.

Sacred 02

Naranja Payphone #FF6400

This specific shade of orange is non-negotiable. Not a brighter orange. Not a darker orange. Not "coral" or "tangerine" or "burnt sienna." #FF6400 is the brand color. It was chosen deliberately and it has been associated with Payphone across millions of touchpoints. Changing it would be like Coca-Cola switching from red.

Sacred 03

Brand Essence: Autonomy

Payphone may change what products it offers, what markets it serves, and how it communicates. It will never change what it stands for. Autonomy — the belief that people already have the power and just need the infrastructure to use it — is the philosophical foundation. Every product must pass the autonomy test: does this make the user more self-sufficient, or more dependent on Payphone?

Sacred 04

Zero-Cost Positioning

The promise that Payphone is zero-cost to the individual user is a brand promise, not just a pricing decision. If the business model evolves to include user fees, the brand narrative must be rebuilt from scratch — which is why the business model should be designed to never require it. This promise is load-bearing.

The Three-Question Brand Test

Before any piece of communication ships — an ad, a post, a deck slide, an email, a product screen — it must pass this three-question test. If any answer is "no," the work goes back for revision.

Question 01

Could this come from any other fintech?

If you replaced "Payphone" with "Mercado Pago" or "Yape" or "Nu" and the piece still made sense, it is not specific enough. Payphone content must contain at least one element (claim, data point, visual, or tone) that is ownable only by Payphone.

If yes (could come from anyone): Find the specific. Add the data point. Name the product. Show the real screen. Make it impossible to swap the brand name.

Question 02

Does this increase autonomy?

Does this piece of communication help the audience become more self-sufficient? Does it teach them something? Does it remove a barrier? Does it give them a tool? Content that only promotes Payphone without increasing the audience's autonomy violates the brand essence.

If no: Reframe from "what Payphone does" to "what you can now do." The subject of the sentence should be the audience, not the brand.

Question 03

Would a Tier 1 VC forward this?

This is the quality bar. Not "is this good enough?" but "is this so good that a busy, skeptical, seen-everything investor would take 15 seconds to forward it to a partner?" If the answer is no, raise the standard. Shorten the copy. Sharpen the insight. Add the number. Remove the filler.

If no: The piece is not done. Ship nothing that would not survive the inbox of the most discerning reader in your audience.
